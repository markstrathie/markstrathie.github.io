COLLECTING DATA

Data can come from many sources such as a census, interviews, observations, forms, questionnaires, or internet cookies. When undertaking your project, you need to decide if you are going to collect new data or use existing data. In our example, and for most portfolio projects, you will work with pre-existing data. You can collect a variety of datasets and merge them to increase the scope of your analysis. Good locations to find public data that is free to use are:

  • Google Dataset Search
  • Kaggle
  • UK Government Website

DATA STRUCTURE

Data can be stored in a large variety of ways. Data can be unstructured such as in the case of a collection of emails or social media posts. This is much harder to organise and analyse and takes more time to transform into useful information. Usually, you will work with structured data in the form of a spreadsheet or database which is the case with our music analysis. Our lyrics are an example of qualitative data, which is longer-form information about a topic. We can use this to make word clouds or perform a sentiment analysis. Our sales information is qualitative data, which is countable, limited values, and can be plotted on a graph and compared.

Data structure is important as it allows us to easily sort and filter for the information we need. Because our music data is organised into album name and total sales/streams, we can sort it in descending order to find out which album was the most/least successful financially. We can then compare the lyrical analysis of each album in an attempt to find relationships between successful musical themes. If the data was not structured we would be unable to do this.

OUR MUSIC DATA

From the planning stage we ascertained 5 important questions we would like to explore:

  1. On a scale of 1-10 (with 10 being the most important) how relevant are song lyrics to an album's success?
  2. What are the top 5 lyrical themes that influence a successful album?
  3. What song elements would make listeners more likely to stream the song?
  4. Have certain themes become more or less popular in the last three years?
  5. How much would a listener pay for a full album instead of consuming via streaming services?

And the root question using the 5 Whys method was:

  • Why is Kanye West's content popular and controversial?

In order to answer these questions, we need to find a dataset containing all of the lyrics for his albums. This will allow us to analyse the themes of his music. We can then compare these themes to current culture to identify anything controversial. In order to measure success/sales/streams we also need a dataset containing album sales/streams. Once we find these 2 datasets we will have enough information to analyse.

If you wanted to take this project further you could analyse another successful artist in order to compare themes and success between them. Kaggle has many datasets of sales figures and datasets of lyrics, including those from Drake, Eminem, Taylor Swift, and Pink Floyd.

OUR DATASETS

Lyrics from 9 out of 10 of Kanye West's studio albums (excluding collaboration albums) can be downloaded from Kaggle at Kanye West Lyrics Dataset. This is already structured in a txt file which we can read with Python. More on installing Python under the tech stack section.

Sales figures can be found from ChartMasters who compiled Kanye West album sales and streams together. We made a downloadable spreadsheet (.csv) file for ease of use. For your own project you may find a dataset already in this format. This data was last updated 03/13/24. Download Kanye West sales spreadsheet.

We recommend storing these in a project folder where you will keep all the files related to your project. This could be in your Documents folder and named 'music-trend-analysis' for example. Make sure your datasets and other files are named appropriately to ensure anyone can easily understand what they are.
