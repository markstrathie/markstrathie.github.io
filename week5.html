Week 5 - Writing characteristics and adapting text

IL Task 1 - Review professional writing characteristics

I read over the following:

1. ACCURACY Inaccuracies show a carelessness that few professionals or organisations can afford in a competitive, global marketplace. Attention to accuracy is therefore paramount to web writers.
2. ACTIVE VOICE Avoid using passive voice construction when you can, unless you need to obscure the person committing an action; e.g.:
  • Passive Voice: The website was written by me.
  • Active Voice: I wrote the website.
3. LIMIT BIASES
  • Use they, instead of he/she to avoid gender bias.
  • Beware of stereotypes to avoid biases, e.g.:
    • Age bias: When you’re over 60, climbing stairs is always painful.
    • Racial bias: The website is available only in English, thus unfortunately only accessible to UK nationals.
    • Implicit bias: Welcome to Pretty Pink, a website dedicated to girls’ favourite subject: languages and literature!
4. CLARITY Use words that your audience will recognise, and use them correctly.
Be careful of jargon and technical terminology, and provide definitions if necessary.
5. CONCISENESS Avoid wordiness – web readers’ impatience is documented and scientifically proven!
6. CONVERSATIONAL AND SMOOTHLY FLOWING PROSE
  • Use simple, plain language in sentences that are not complex or convoluted.
  • Use contractions when appropriate (e.g. I’ll instead of I will).
  • Against school learning, starting with conjunctions (e.g. and, but) gives text the right conversational tone.
7. CORRECTNESS Poor grammar, and words used incorrectly, make both the writer and the organisation appear ignorant and sloppy.
Common mistakes:
  • It’s = It is >> It’s late = It is late. Its = possessive pronoun, something belonging to “it” >>
    The dog is chasing its tail (i.e. the tail belonging to the dog)
  • Effect = noun >> Paracetamol has a positive effect on headaches.
    Affect = verb >> Paracetamol can affect people’s stomach.
8. PARALLELISM
  • Parallelism is a writing technique that appeals to our natural desire for symmetry. Parallelism matches nouns with nouns, verbs with verbs, and phrases with phrases: “For faster action, less pain, and more for your money, use XX.”
  • Readers expect parallelism, especially in sets of two or three items. Using parallel phrasing sets up an expectation, and when used correctly, delivers a punch.
9. POSITIVE VOICE Be mindful of downbeat words or words that have a negative connotation. If necessary, rephrase in a positive way. E.g.:
  • No entries to the competition are allowed after the 1st November. (emphasises restrictions and a missed opportunity)
  • Entries to the competition are allowed until the 31st October. (emphasises permission and opportunity)
10. STRONG NOUNS AND VERBS
  • Use nouns and verbs to do the heavy work, and save adverbs and adjectives for necessary emphasis. E.g.:
    • Our website offers you high-quality, accurate news content.

      • Instead of…

    • Our amazing website is your only and most trustworthy source for the highest quality, and of exquisite accuracy, news content.
  • Check for overuse of the verb to be (is, are, were, was, has been etc.) and see if you can use a stronger, more specific verb instead. E.g.:
    • The competition starts on the 1st October.

      • Instead of…

    • The start date for the competition is the 1st October.
11. SENTENCE VARIETY
  • Avoid writing sentences that begin with the same word or phrase. (There is, There are, As mentioned before are common culprits!)
  • Vary sentence length combining short, medium and longer sentences.
12. SIMPLE WORDS Prefer more recognisable words over showy words. E.g.:
  • Email instead of electronic message
  • The increase in unemployment was the result of expenditure cuts. instead of… The increase in unemployment was the corollary of expenditure cuts.
13. SHORTER PARAGRAPHS Long paragraphs are maybe good for essays, but can scare web readers! Opt for no more than 6-8 lines for web paragraphs – remember the research on visitors’ attention span.
14. TONE AND VOICE Adjust your writing attitude to the task at hand. Tone and voice are tied to the intended audience and the purpose of a website. E.g.:
  • Some web content will require a formal voice, which is controlled and objective – e.g. information on taxation rules in gov.uk web page.
  • Other content may call for an informal tone in which you can be more personal and casual, e.g. a personal blog of your travels.

IL Task 2 - Evaluate resources

I chose the Apple UK website to evaluate against the 14 professional writing characteristics.

1. ACCURACY This is the manufacturer's website so it's expected to be accurate and informative about its own products. It succeeds in this regard by providing a page of technical specifications for people to check that want more detail or to check the facts - apple.com/uk/iphone-15-pro/specs
2. ACTIVE VOICE Active voice is used successfully to build a connection with the customer. Words like 'we' and 'our' are used to refer the the brand. The reader is addressed as 'you', as if Apple are having a conversations with the customer.
3. LIMIT BIASES
    Apple are sucessful in creating text that feels personal with sentences like 'taking a photo at your daughter's football match,' without relying on any sterotypes or biases. They also have the option to select your region, making it available to many non-English speakers:

4. CLARITY "iPhone 15 Pro is the first iPhone to feature an aerospace‑grade titanium design, using the same alloy that spacecraft use for missions to Mars." As seen here, Apple provide clarity on the material of titanium. Instead of lauching into jargon about it's physical properties, they compare it to the spacecraft used in NASA's missions to Mars, something that the majority of people will be familiar with.
5. CONCISENESS As shown below, Apple breaks up its text into concise chunks that are easy to digest by using many small paragraphs and lists. This makes it easier for the reader to pick out information they deem relevant in the phone for them.

6. CONVERSATIONAL AND SMOOTHLY FLOWING PROSE
    Apple successfully using contractions and sentences beginning with 'And' to acheive an informal and personal tone. All their sentences are arranged in small paragraphs making the text flow smoothly.
7. CORRECTNESS No evident spelling or grammer mistakes.
8. PARALLELISM "Enter A17 Pro. Game‑changing chip. Groundbreaking performance." Parallelism is used to deliver a punch on the key features.
9. POSITIVE VOICE When discussing battery life, the performance is positively emphasised - 'Up to 29 hrs.' They do not say 'Only 29 hrs' or 'The battery will run out after 29hrs.' This technique is used throughout.
10. STRONG NOUNS AND VERBS Apple uses simple but bold sentences like "A camera that captures your wildest imagination."
11. SENTENCE VARIETY They begin paragraphs and sentences with a variety of phrases, questions and words.
12. SIMPLE WORDS Apple prioritises simple words over showy words. Any technical or more complicated terms are always explained with a simile or additional information.
13. SHORTER PARAGRAPHS Text is used sparingly in combination with videos and large pictures to keep the readers' attentions.
14. TONE AND VOICE Tone is kept professional but friendly.

Lab Task 2 - Create Samples of text

My target audience are my peer group of students or young professionals seeking a career in the tech industry. Predominantly male, 20-30 years old.

Key points from my narrative are the opening scene - introducing data science. And introducing AI towards the end of the narrative. I will write a sample paragraph for each below:

  • We are surrounded by more data than we can comprehend. The average internet user creates over 1.7 megabytes per second. This amounts to more than 40 trillion gigabytes per year.

    All this data is extremely valuable to organisations who can use it to make data-driven decisions – using the data to provide insights into how successful their services are, how to improve upon them, and drive up their profits.

    However, specialists are essential for this task. Because internet users simply go about their daily business on a variety of devices from mobiles to laptops. They interact with different websites and platforms. The result is that 80-90% of data is unstructured and not valuable in this form. Before insights can be provided, data needs to be collected, stored, cleaned and analysed. This is where the role of the Data Scientist comes in.

    There’s such an abundance of data, and a shortage of skilled data scientists, that the early stages of collecting to analysing data provide substantial value compared to previous decision-making methods. Once much of the insights provided by this process have been exhausted, the new methods of deep learning and Artificial Intelligence can be utilised.

  • Now that we have explored the fundamentals of data science, we should turn our attention to what AI tools are currently used in the industry. Many aspects of data science are increasingly being automated using automated machine learning to improve productivity. This is called AutoML. And once analytic models have been developed, their accuracy is monitored by machine learning operations tools called MLOps.

    There are also large-language models (LLMs) such as ChatGPT. These tools have the ability to create code, saving us time from writing out boilerplate and other simple code. ChatGPT can be used as a ‘data assistant,’ providing us with advice on how to define data structures, arrange charts and suggest information for reports.

    An important issue we need to be aware of when using these tools is AI ethics. Chat-GPT can help provide us with guidelines on how to make sure our use of AI doesn’t violate privacy terms, reinforce biases and is used sustainably.
